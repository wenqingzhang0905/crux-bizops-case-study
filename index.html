01 Prioritization + Discovery
How to prioritize and what to ask first

Where are the delays?

Start with the stage that creates the most delays, takes the longest, and has the biggest downstream impact. That's due diligence.

Where does delay carry cost?

Delay can be financially costly. Operational shortfalls in close are not scheduling friction, when do they have direct commercial impact?

Which fix enables the most?

Structured intake improves every downstream stage. HubSpot as SSOT makes dashboards possible. Get the foundation right first.

Intake + HubSpot → DD workspace → IC prep → Reporting

First two weeks: what I would ask

  1. Where do deals stall most? Has an operational issue ever caused a real delay or loss?
  2. Who owns each deal at each stage, and is that universally understood?
  3. Which deal stage does "the most effort" go?
  4. What does leadership most wish it could see that it currently cannot?
  5. Who on the team would want to help design the new system?

I'd also shadow 2–3 deal reviews and map where deals actually live today.

02 Automation Strategy
Automate the routine to elevate human judgment
Automate anything routine and deterministic. Keep humans in the loop for anything that requires judgment or carries real consequences.

Automate (n8n)

  • Deal record creation on intake form submission
  • Notion workspace generation on DD entry
  • Stage transition notifications to incoming owner
  • Committee calendar hold + memo template setup
  • Stale-deal alerts (no activity >7 days)
  • Monday pipeline snapshot auto-distribution

Human Judgment

  • Evaluating deal viability
  • Synthesizing risk across five DD workstreams
  • Term structuring and pricing decisions
  • Investment Committee approval vote
  • Co-investor and partnership relationship management
  • Evaluating investment criteria
03 Cross-Functional Collaboration
The Right Channel for the Right Person

Co-investors

Keep them informed, not overwhelmed. Shared data room linked from HubSpot with milestone updates.

Legal counsel

Legal is the critical path. Treat their timeline as the constraint and build coordination around it.

External consultants

We can give them exactly what they need such as a Notion workstream tab. Their updates flow back to HubSpot without them ever logging into it (with n8n).

Sponsors

This stays human and tailored. The system makes sure the team never walks into a sponsor call without a current picture of where the deal stands.

Internal rhythm

Every stage transition fires a notification to the incoming owner, so no one has to chase. Monday review is 30 minutes, agenda auto-generated. That meeting is for real collaboration and decisions, not figuring out where things stand.

The goal is to enhance how the team coordinates, using the right tool helps teams collaborate better
03 Adoption
Earn adoption. Don't mandate it.
The best process is one people actually use. That means co-designing it with them, not rolling it out to them.

Shadow before you build

Two weeks of watching before I touch anything. I want to understand how deals move , and how people would love things to operate. The gap between those two things is where the real problems are.

Find the person who feels the pain most

Through multiple rounds of conversations, find the one who would genuinely benefit and build with them. Their credibility becomes the system's credibility. People adopt things that feel like theirs.

Build and ship something small first

A shared pipeline view people can see in week 3 does more for adoption than a perfect system in month 3. Start with the most complained-about problem. The system works when it makes people's jobs easier, not when it adds steps to their day.

Keep the feedback loop visible

If something isn't working, I want to know early, not after people have quietly stopped using it. I'd make it easy to flag friction and make it visible when something changes because of that feedback. People stay engaged with a system that listens back.

Using Data to Iterate: What "Good" Looks Like

The system isn't finished when it launches. It's finished when the team stops noticing it because it just works. Data tells us when we're getting closer and when something needs to change.

How I'd use data to iterate

  • Track adoption, not just usage. Are people entering deals through the intake form, or still emailing spreadsheets? If adoption stalls, the system has a friction problem, not a training problem.
  • Watch for workarounds. If someone builds a side spreadsheet to track DD status, that's a signal the Notion workspace isn't giving them what they need. I'd interview them and redesign that piece.
  • Measure what the system was built to fix. Stage velocity, stall frequency, time-to-close. If DD cycle time isn't improving after 8 weeks, I'd dig into which workstream is still blocking and whether automation is actually reaching the right people.
  • Review stall alert accuracy. Are alerts firing too early (noise) or too late (useless)? I'd tune thresholds quarterly based on actual deal patterns.
  • Use Monday reviews as a feedback loop. If the team is still spending the first 10 minutes getting oriented, the snapshot isn't working. If they jump straight to decisions, it is.

At 3 months: "The system is real"

The bar is adoption and reliability, not perfection.
  • Every active deal lives in HubSpot with accurate stage and owner
  • DD workspaces auto-generate and consultants are using them
  • Stall alerts have fired and at least one has prevented a real delay
  • Monday review prep takes <5 minutes instead of 30+
  • Leadership can pull up pipeline health without asking anyone

At 12 months: "We can't imagine going back"

The bar is compounding value and predictive capability.
  • Enough historical data to benchmark: average close time by deal type, conversion rates by sponsor tier, DD bottleneck patterns
  • The system is predictive, not just reactive: flagging at-risk deals based on patterns, not just missed deadlines
  • Pipeline capacity planning is data-driven: we know how many deals the team can realistically handle in parallel
  • New team members onboard faster because the system documents how deals actually move
  • The system has been iterated 3-4 times based on real usage data, and each iteration was driven by a specific metric, not a guess
The difference between 3 months and 12 months isn't more features. It's compounding insight. At 3 months the system tells you where things stand. At 12 months it tells you where things are going.
